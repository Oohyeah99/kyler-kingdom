Why We Split Words This Way

This system is designed to help young children (ages 5–8) read new words quickly and confidently.

Instead of following complex dictionary rules, we use a simple, consistent method that helps children break words into manageable parts, recognize patterns faster, and build strong reading instincts.

Why Not Use Traditional Syllable Rules?

Traditional rules are based on linguistics and word history — not how children learn. They're often inconsistent and confusing for beginners.

For example: "visit" → vis-it (dictionary) but a child naturally sees → vi-sit

This mismatch creates unnecessary confusion.

Our Approach: A Decoding Strategy

We teach children a decoding strategy, not a dictionary system. We prioritize simplicity, consistency, and speed of learning.

Even if the split is not "official," it helps children read correctly, which is the real goal.

The Core Idea

Each syllable should start with a consonant whenever possible.

This makes words easier to chunk, pronounce, and remember.

Rule 1: One Consonant → Move It Forward

basic → ba-sic   visit → vi-sit   lemon → le-mon

Rule 2: Two Consonants → Split Them

happy → hap-py   basket → bas-ket

Rule 3: Keep Blends Together

table → ta-ble   apron → a-pron

Rule 4: "x" Splits Before

exit → ex-it   exam → ex-am

Important: Sound Comes After Splitting

Splitting does NOT decide pronunciation. Children are taught to "look ahead and choose the correct vowel sound."

Examples: vi-sit → "vis-it"  |  ba-sic → "bay-sic"

"We are not teaching children to analyze English. We are teaching them to read it."
